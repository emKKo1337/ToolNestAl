## What Makes a Business Name Truly Memorable

Before you generate anything, it helps to know what you are aiming for. The strongest brand names share six qualities:

**1. Short and pronounceable.** Names under 10 characters that can be said clearly out loud win on every front — word of mouth, search recall, logo design, social handles. "Nexlify" beats "NextLevelifyPlatform" every time.

**2. Unique and ownable.** A name that exists as a common word or phrase is hard to trademark and harder to rank for. The best brand names are invented or repurposed in a way that makes them distinctly yours — Spotify, Slack, Notion.

**3. Domain available.** A .com domain is still the default trust signal. If the .com is taken, .io and .co are credible alternatives for tech and SaaS companies. Check before you get attached.

**4. Niche-relevant without being limiting.** A name should hint at what you do without boxing you in as you grow. "Amazon" started as a bookshop. "Apple" sells computers. The best names leave room.

**5. Emotionally resonant.** Great names evoke something — speed, trust, warmth, creativity, movement. Names that feel neutral are forgettable. Names that feel like something stick.

**6. Scalable.** Avoid names tied to a specific city, product feature or trend. What works for your first product should still work when your tenth product ships three years later.

## Common Naming Mistakes to Avoid

**Choosing a name that is already taken.** This sounds obvious but founders regularly fall in love with a name before checking trademark databases. Always search before you announce.

**Making it too literal.** "London Cupcakes" tells customers exactly what you sell today — and locks you in if you want to expand to other cities or products. Great names gesture at what you do without spelling it out.

**Prioritising cleverness over clarity.** Puns, portmanteaus and wordplay can be charming, but if someone mishears your name or cannot spell it after hearing it, you are losing referrals before you start.

**Ignoring how the name looks as a URL.** Some names create unintended readings when the spaces are removed. Check the URL version — whorepresents.com and speedofart.com are cautionary tales.

**Forgetting international audiences.** If you plan to sell globally, check that your chosen name does not mean something awkward in another language. Run it past a native speaker of your target markets.

**Skipping the social handle check.** A .com registration means nothing if @yourbrand is taken on every major platform by someone else. Check handles before you commit.

**Settling too fast.** The first batch of names you generate is rarely the best. Adjust keywords, change the tone, try a different length setting. The tool is free and unlimited — use it that way.
